# India's 1991 Economic Crisis

## Factors That Caused It

1. **BOP Crisis:** The volume of India's imports were much greater than its exports, leading to a BOP crisis. This deficit had to be overcome by borrowing from international lenders. India was on the verge of sovereign default. The IMF refused to offer financial assistance to India unless they liberalized the country.

2. **Gulf War and Oil Price Shock:** As India imports most of its oil requirements, when the price of oil rose in 1991 due to the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, the foreign import bill drastically increased. There were a significant number of Indian workers in these countries, and when the war started, they had to be evacuated. This caused the loss of both the remittances they had been sending home, and put an additional burden on the exchequer as they had to bring them back home.

3. **Fiscal Deficit and Excessive Government Spending:** Various Indian governments tried to implement large scale infrastructure projects, defence spending, public sector wages, etc. However, instead of financing this with tax revenue, they borrowed from international lenders. As the deficit grew, the government had to borrow more and more to pay its previous loans. The RBI tried to cut the deficit by printing more money, but this caused inflation. Inefficient PSEs added to the burden by running at a loss.

4. **License Raj and Structural Economic Stagnation:** India used to be a socialist country. There was a system called the License Raj wherein any business activity required government approval in the form of permits, licenses and quotas. Domestic industries were shielded from foreign competition by heavy import duties. However, due to the lack of competition, Indian industries were inefficient and unable to compete with foreign companies. As a result, the growth rate was extremely menial.

5. **Political Instability and Policy Paralysis:** After the Congress party lost power in 1989, there were 3 successive governments in 3 years. There were many coalition arrangements, which prevented one party from having a clear majority. This prevented these governments from pursuing reforms due to the fear of backlash and lack of authority. The assassination of Rajiv Gandhi in May 1991 added to the instability.

6. **Rising External Debt and Credit Rating Downgrade:** By 1991, due to excessive borrowing, India's foreign debt had risen to a huge level. This made foreign lenders cautious of lending further. International credit agencies downgraded India's credit rating, meaning that India was deemed a high risk borrower. This caused foreign investors to exit India and NRIs to withdraw their deposits from Indian banks, leading to a depletion of forex reserves.

---

## India's Response

1. **Emergency Gold Pledge:** The newly elected government of Narasimha Rao secretly airlifted 47 tonnes of gold totally to the Bank of England and Union Bank of Switzerland. This raised about $600 million.

2. **IMF Loan:** When India approached the IMF for a loan, they agreed, but with several conditionals: India had to reduce the fiscal deficit, liberalize trade, devalue the currency, and undertake structural reforms to open the economy to private and foreign investment.

3. **Devaluation of the Rupee:** On July 1, 1991, the rupee was devalued by approximately 9% against major currencies. Just two days later, on July 3, a second devaluation took the total depreciation to about 18–19%. This was done so that exports would become more attractive to foreign countries due to being relatively cheaper. More exports would raise the forex reserves.

4. **Emergency Budget:** Manmohan Singh presented the Union Budget of 1991. It was designed to reduce government spending, increase government revenue from taxes, and cut the fiscal deficit.

5. **NEP:** India implemented the New Economic Policy on July 24, 1991. India dismantled the License Raj and introduced the policies of LPG (Liberalization, Privatization and Globalization). The Monopolies and Restrictive Trade Practices Act was amended.

---

## Key Policies Under Liberalization

1. **Industrial Policy Reforms:** The License Raj was abolished, dereservation was implemented, and the MRTP Act was amended to relax restrictions on large monopolies.

2. **Trade Policy Reforms:** Imports were liberalized and tariffs were abolished, leading to competition between foreign and Indian companies. Many policies such as the devaluation of the rupee were introduced to encourage exports.

3. **Financial Sector Reforms:** Private and foreign banks were allowed to operate more freely; interest rates were gradually deregulated. SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) was strengthened to regulate and develop the stock market. FDI limits were raised in key sectors, and Foreign Institutional Investors (FIIs) were allowed to invest in Indian markets.

4. **Fiscal Reforms:** Income tax rates were reduced and the tax system simplified to encourage compliance and investment. Subsidies were rationalized to cut down the fiscal deficit. The government practiced disinvestment — it sold its stake in PSEs to raise revenue and turn loss-making PSEs into private companies.

5. **Exchange Rate Policies:** A market-determined exchange rate system was gradually introduced, moving away from the fixed rate regime. Current account convertibility of the rupee was achieved by 1994, making trade transactions easier.
