 The Business ofSport: How the EPL, IPL, and NBA Make (and Split) Their Money


Overview
Three leagues. Three sports. Three continents. The English Premier League, the Indian Premier League, and the National Basketball Association are among the most commercially powerful sports organizations on earth — yet they operate on fundamentally diﬀerent ﬁnancial philosophies. The EPL distributes nearly all its central revenues to clubs. The IPL funnels half upward to its governing board. The NBA ties player pay directly to a shared income pool.
Understanding how each makes money, what drives their value, and who gets what cut reveals just how varied the economics ofelite sport can be.


Part I: The English Premier League (EPL)
Total Scale
The Premier League is the richest football league in the world. In the 2023/24 season, the 20 clubs collectively generated approximately £6.3 billion in aggregate revenue — more than La Liga, Bundesliga, and Serie A combined. For 2024/25, aggregate revenue is projected to grow to roughly £6.5–6.7 billion, driven by a new broadcast cycle and surging commercial income.


Revenue Streams
Broadcast Rights — The Dominant Engine
Broadcasting is by far the largest revenue source for the Premier League, accounting for roughly
£3.3 billion per year to clubs in 2023/24.
The Premier League negotiates broadcast deals centrally on behalfofall 20 clubs — a model that has made it uniquely powerful. Rights are sold in two pools:
Domestic rights (2025–2029 cycle): A landmark four-year deal worth £6.7 billion total (≈ £1.67 billion/season), the largest sports media rights deal in UK history. Sky Sports dominates this cycle, paying for 215 matches per season — a 70% increase from its previous allocation. The deal is the most expensive on a per-game basis ofany league globally outside the NFL, at roughly
£13.8 million per match.
International rights (2025–2028 cycle): For the ﬁrst time in the league's history, international revenue has overtaken domestic — bringing in approximately £2.17 billion per season. The US

market alone (NBC Sports) pays roughly £378 million annually. The league broadcasts to 900 million homes in 189 countries, giving it unmatched global reach. Premier League games regularly attract more US viewers than many NBA regular-season games — a Liverpool vs Manchester United match in March 2024 drew 2.6 million American viewers.

Commercial Partnerships & Sponsorships
The Premier League operates a compact but highly exclusive portfolio of seven oﬃcial partners and ﬁve oﬃcial licensees. Its lead partner, EA Sports, holds a deal reportedly worth close to
£500 million for the 2023–2029 period. Guinness replaced Budweiser as oﬃcial beer partner in 2024, paying approximately £52 million through 2028 — nearly double what Budweiser was paying.
At the club level, total sponsorship value across all 20 clubs reached £1.58 billion in 2024/25 — a 9% year-on-year increase. Manchester United alone has the largest sponsorship portfolio, valued at approximately $327 million. Shirt sponsorships for the "Big Six" clubs can be worth up to £60 million per year, while mid-table clubs typically earn around £10 million annually from front-of-shirt deals. Sleeve patch deals add a further £500,000–£10 million per club.
Total club commercial revenue (sponsorships, merchandise, tours, licensing) reached £2.15 billion in 2023/24 and is projected to reach £2.3 billion in 2024/25.

Matchday Revenue
Gate receipts, hospitality, food, beverages, and stadium events collectively crossed £900 million for the ﬁrst time in 2023/24, with 2024/25 expected to approach £1 billion. This revenue is entirely club-retained — the league takes no cut. Matchday earnings are highly unequal: clubs with large, modern stadiums (Arsenal's Emirates, Manchester United's Old Traﬀord, Tottenham's new ground) earn dramatically more than smaller clubs.

UEFA Prize Money
Clubs that qualify for UEFA's Champions League, Europa League, or Conference League earn additional distributions from UEFA. Champions League participants receive a base fee of €15.64 million, with further payments for wins, advancement through rounds, and a coeﬃcient payment based on historical European performance. For 2024/25, UEFA distributions are expected to grow signiﬁcantly due to a new expanded format.


How Is the Money Split?
The Premier League distributes virtually all ofits central broadcast and commercial revenues to clubs (retaining a relatively small operational amount). The distribution formula has three components:

Equal Share: Every club receives the same base amount, regardless ofperformance. For 2024/25:
£29.8 million from domestic rights and £59.2 million from international rights — a total equal share of £89 million per club.
Merit Payments: Based on ﬁnal league position. For 2024/25, each place in the table was worth approximately £1.6 million from domestic rights and £1.04 million from international rights. Champions Liverpool received roughly £53 million in merit payments; last-placed Southampton received about £2.6 million.
Facility Fees: Paid to clubs when their matches are selected for live domestic broadcast. Liverpool's Champions League-winning season earned them £24.9 million in facility fees; Southampton earned £9.7 million.
Central commercial payment: Each club also receives an equal share ofleague-level sponsorship revenue — £7.9 million per club in 2024/25, derived from deals with EA Sports, Guinness, Barclays, Coca-Cola, Adobe, and others.
Bottom line for 2024/25: Even the last-placed team (Southampton) received approximately
£106.7 million in total central distributions. The champions (Liverpool) received approximately
£176.4 million. The spread is intentionally compressed — this relative parity is what allows mid-table clubs to pay £40 million for a single player and compete with continental giants.
Solidarity payments: A portion of Premier League revenue is also redistributed downward to the English Football League (EFL) and grassroots football. The EFL's separate Sky Sports deal (£935 million over ﬁve years, from 2024) supports the 72 clubs in Leagues One, Two, and the Championship, who also receive "parachute payments" when relegated from the top ﬂight.
Club-level revenues (matchday, their own sponsorships, commercial deals) stay entirely with the individual clubs. This is where inequality really compounds: Manchester United's £327 million sponsorship portfolio dwarfs that ofa club like Brentford.


What Makes the EPLValuable?
Global language advantage: English gives the league a built-in international audience no other football league can match.
Competitive depth: The equal-share broadcast model means even mid-table clubs can attract world-class players, producing genuine unpredictability.
American investment: US private equity now owns 9 of 20 clubs, bringing NFL-style commercial thinking. Chelsea was sold for £4.25 billion; Crystal Palace for £2.5 billion.
Star power with global touchpoints: Mohamed Salah created an Egyptian fanbase; Takehiro Tomiyasu brings Japanese eyeballs; Gabriel Jesus reaches Brazil. Each signing is also a market.



Part II: The Indian Premier League (IPL)
Total Scale
Founded in 2008, the IPL has grown with remarkable speed into the world's second-richest sports league by per-match value, behind only the NFL. The BCCI's total revenue for ﬁnancial year 2024 was ₹20,686 crore (~$2.5 billion), a ₹4,200 crore increase from the previous year. The IPL's brand value in 2024 was estimated at $3.4 billion, while the overall league valuation — including franchise values — reached $12 billion.


Revenue Streams
Media Rights — The Crown Jewel
The IPL's media rights auction in 2022 was a watershed moment for global sport. The BCCI sold rights for the 2023–2027 cycle for a staggering ₹48,390 crore (~$6.4 billion), split evenly between:
Star Sports (television): ₹23,575 crore for traditional TV broadcasts.
Viacom18 (digital/streaming): ₹23,575 crore for digital rights on JioCinema. (These entities have since merged into JioStar under Reliance Industries.)

This makes IPL media rights worth approximately $11.3 million per match — surpassing the EPL and trailing only the NFL globally. In the 2024 season, JioCinema estimated that 620 million viewers watched part ofthe IPL, with over 350 billion minutes streamed across the tournament. The 2025 ﬁnal attracted 169 million viewers on linear TV, the most-watched cricket broadcast ever on television.
Rights are sold on a per-match basis, with the understanding that the match count will grow — from 74 matches in 2023/24, to 84 in 2025/26, and 94 in 2027.

Title & Central Sponsorship
Title sponsorship is one ofthe BCCI's most lucrative central assets. Tata Group holds the title rights from 2024 to 2028 for ₹2,500 crore (~$300 million total, or ~₹500 crore/~$60 million per season) — an 11.5x increase from the inaugural DLF deal in 2008. Associate sponsors (RuPay, Angel One, My11Circle, and others) contribute approximately ₹210 crore annually. Smaller branded segments — like the Oﬃcial Strategic Timeout — add a further ₹30 crore or more per season.
Total central sponsorship revenue reached over $100 million per year in 2024, with the BCCI maintaining a deliberately smaller, more stable portfolio after a period ofsponsor churn from the tech/startup sector.

Franchise-Level Sponsorships
Each team independently negotiates its own sponsor stack — a principal partner (front-of-jersey), secondary partners (sleeve, back), and associate partners. Mumbai Indians (MI) command the highest franchise fees globally among cricket teams, valued at $119 million as of 2024. Chennai Super Kings has over 20 individual sponsors, generating over ₹100 crore annually. Front-of-shirt deals for top teams range from $10–20 million per year; smaller franchises like Sunrisers Hyderabad and Punjab Kings earn closer to $2.3 million per year from shirt sponsorships.

Matchday (Ticket Sales & In-Stadium)
Each team plays 7–8 home matches per season. Ticket sales contribute around 10–15% ofa team's total revenue. The split on ticket revenue is: 80% to the home team, 20% shared between the BCCI and other stakeholders (visiting team, sponsors). Food, beverage, and stadium concessions stay almost entirely with the home franchise.

Merchandise
Merchandise — jerseys, caps, ﬂags, memorabilia — is a growing but currently smaller revenue stream for IPL franchises compared to EPL clubs or NBA teams. Franchise expansion into lifestyle products (Mumbai Indians now has a broader consumer brand) and global cricket leagues has begun accelerating this.


How Is the Money Split?
The IPL's distribution model is more centralized than the EPL's, with the BCCI retaining a signiﬁcant cut:
Media rights revenue: The BCCI retains ~50% ofall central revenues (media rights + sponsorship), then distributes:
~45% ofthe central pool is split among the 10 franchises, with the share weighted partly by performance (position at end ofseason).
~5% is allocated as prize money: the winning team receives ₹20 crore, the runner-up ₹13 crore, and the two other playoﬀsemiﬁnalists ₹7 crore each. Prize money is split 50/50 between team owners and players.

Fixed franchise payment: As of 2024/25, each franchise receives a ﬁxed payment of approximately ₹425 crore (~$51 million) per season as their guaranteed share ofcentral revenues.
Ticket revenue: 80% home team, 20% BCCI/others.

Licensing fees: BCCI takes 12.5% ofeach team's licensing revenue in addition to the above.
Franchise fees: Teams pay the BCCI an annual franchise fee simply for the right to operate, eﬀectively a "membership cost" to be in the league.
Key diﬀerence from the EPL: In the EPL, clubs essentially own the product collectively and receive back nearly everything. In the IPL, the BCCI is the product owner — franchises are licensees paying to participate. The BCCI's cut is the structural diﬀerence that makes it look more like a promoter-run entertainment event than a club-owned competition.


What Makes the IPLValuable?
Captive market of1.4 billion: Cricket is a quasi-religion in India; the IPL has near-total penetration among the middle class.
T20 format: 3.5 hours ofcricket packed with boundaries, sixes, and tactical timeouts optimized for advertising inventory. It was engineered as television entertainment from day one.
Star power + Bollywood: The fusion ofcelebrity culture, sports, and ﬁlm has made IPL teams lifestyle brands as much as sports franchises.
Free streaming: JioCinema made IPL free on mobile in 2023, driving astronomical digital viewership and advertising rates. IPL ad rates on Star Sports and digital platforms command a premium per 10-second spot rivalling Super Bowl economics within India.
Global franchise ambitions: IPL team owners are buying stakes in cricket leagues across the UK (The Hundred), USA (MLC), South Africa, UAE, and the Caribbean — turning IPL franchises into global sports holding companies.



Part III: The National Basketball Association (NBA)
Total Scale
The NBA is North America's third-largest sports league by revenue, behind the NFL and MLB. Total league revenue for 2024/25 was approximately $12.25 billion (~$408 million per team on average), ranging from $833 million for the Golden State Warriors to $301 million for the Memphis Grizzlies. For 2025/26, the league projects a record $14.3 billion, a 12% jump driven by the landmark new broadcast deal.


Revenue Streams
National Broadcast Rights — A Seismic New Deal

Until 2024/25, the NBA's national TV deal was worth approximately $2.66 billion per year split between ESPN/ABC and TNT/Warner Bros. Discovery.
Beginning with the 2025/26 season, a new 11-year, $76 billion deal with Disney (ABC/ESPN), NBCUniversal (NBC/Peacock), and Amazon Prime Video takes eﬀect — running through 2035/36. The breakdown per broadcaster:
Disney (ABC/ESPN): ~$2.6 billion/year — retains the NBA Finals and one conference ﬁnal.
NBCUniversal (NBC/Peacock): ~$2.5 billion/year — airs All-Star Weekend, Sunday night games, and alternates the second conference ﬁnal with Amazon.
Amazon Prime Video: ~$1.8 billion/year — Thursday nights, the In-Season Tournament, and one conference ﬁnal (alternating).

Total average: $6.9 billion/season — a 2.6x increase over the prior deal. Each team's share of national TV revenue rises from $103 million to $143 million per season, with annual increases ofroughly 7% built in going forward.

Local Broadcast & Regional Sports Networks (RSNs)
In addition to national deals, NBA teams negotiate their own local TV contracts with regional sports networks. This is a major revenue diﬀerentiator between markets. The Lakers hold the NBA's richest local deal — their agreement with Charter/Spectrum paid the club approximately
$200 million for 2024/25 alone. Small-market teams earn comparatively little here, which is partly why the revenue-sharing system exists.
(Note: The RSNmarket has experienced signiﬁcant disruption, with several regional networks going bankrupt or droppingsports rights, which depressed overall BRIslightly in 2023/24.)
Gate Receipts & Arena Revenue
Ticket sales across 30 teams represent approximately 30% oftotal revenue. This includes courtside seats (which can cost $10,000+ per game at marquee franchises), suites, club seating, and general admission. Game-day income also includes concessions, parking, and merchandise sold in-arena.
Beyond basketball, NBA arenas are increasingly run as year-round businesses. Eleven NBA arenas grossed at least $100 million in ticket revenue from concerts alone in 2024/25, according to Billboard — with teams capturing concession, parking, and suite income from those events even as most ticket revenue goes to the artists.

Sponsorships
The NBA generated over $1.6 billion in total sponsorship revenue in 2024. The league has 32 active central sponsors, collectively worth approximately $893.8 million for 2024/25. Financial services led all sectors with $247 million invested, followed by technology ($122 million) and healthcare ($105 million).

At the team level, jersey patch sponsorships (introduced in 2017/18) have become a signiﬁcant revenue stream. Patch deals vary enormously by market — top franchises earn $20–30 million per year from jersey patch partners. The Hornets, Spurs, and Clippers had no patch partner as of 2024/25.

Merchandise & Licensing
The NBA licenses its brand, team logos, and player likenesses to apparel manufacturers, video game developers, and merchandise retailers globally. Nike holds the NBA's exclusive apparel manufacturing and on-court uniform deal, worth approximately $1 billion over its term, with a royalty structure that ﬂows back to the league and teams. International merchandise and licensing — especially in Asia and Europe — has grown signiﬁcantly as the NBA has expanded its global footprint. The league dominates all US sports in global social media reach, which drives merchandise demand internationally.

NBA League Pass & Digital
The NBA operates its own streaming subscription product (NBA League Pass), which allows fans globally to watch out-of-market games. While smaller in absolute dollar terms than the broadcast deals, League Pass contributes meaningfully to digital revenue and international fan engagement.


How Is the Money Split?
The NBA has the most structurally sophisticated revenue-sharing model ofthe three leagues, with a direct contractual link between revenues and player pay.
Basketball Related Income (BRI): All signiﬁcant revenue streams — national media, ticketing, sponsorships, merchandise, and licensing — are aggregated into a deﬁned pool called Basketball Related Income. For 2023/24, total BRI was approximately $10.25 billion.
The 51/49 split: Under the Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA), players are entitled to approximately 51% ofBRI. Owners retain 49%. This is not a proﬁt split — it's a revenue split that directly funds total player salaries across all 30 teams. In 2023/24, this shortfall caused players to return approximately $484 million to teams via escrow to maintain the agreed split.
Salary cap: The BRI split determines the NBA salary cap — the maximum each team can spend on player payroll. The cap is set annually based on projected BRI. With the new broadcast deal boosting BRI signiﬁcantly, salary caps are rising by up to 10% annually (a smoothing limit is in place to avoid the 2016-style distortion when cap jumped $24 million in one summer).
National TV revenue distribution: National broadcast revenue is divided equally among all 30 teams. For 2025/26, each team receives approximately $143 million from national TV money — up from $103 million the prior season.

Revenue sharing: Roughly $400 million per year is redistributed from high-revenue to low-revenue teams, funded by a combination ofthe wealthiest franchises and 50% ofluxury tax proceeds. This helps small-market teams like Memphis and Oklahoma City remain ﬁnancially viable.
Luxury tax: Teams that exceed the salary cap pay a punitive luxury tax on every dollar over the threshold. Tax proceeds are partially distributed to non-taxpaying teams, further compressing the competitive gap.
Local revenues stay local: Unlike national TV money, local broadcast deals, gate receipts, and team-level sponsorships stay with individual franchises — subject to the revenue-sharing top-up. This is why the Warriors earned $833 million against the Grizzlies' $301 million.


What Makes the NBA Valuable?
Global stars as global brands: LeBron James, Stephen Curry, and Giannis Antetokounmpo are not just basketball players — they are global cultural ﬁgures. The NBA has long understood that marketing individual athletes grows the overall pie.
The new broadcast deal: At $76 billion over 11 years, this is one ofthe most valuable sports media deals in history. The arrival ofAmazon and NBC alongside ESPN transforms the league's reach into streaming-era media.
International market: The NBA is by far the most-watched US sport internationally, led by China, Europe, and rapidly growing markets in India and Africa. International games (playing regular season in Paris, London, and Abu Dhabi) are expanding this.
WNBA tailwind: The WNBA's explosive growth in viewership and sponsorship interest in 2024/25 has added new value to the NBA's overall portfolio, as both leagues share ownership structures.

Final Thoughts
Each league reﬂects a distinct philosophy. The EPL is a club-driven competition where the product is owned collectively by the teams — making broadcast equity the equalizer. The IPL is a promoter-driven entertainment event where the BCCI remains the majority beneﬁciary and franchises are sophisticated but ultimately licensed operators. The NBA has built the most institutionally sophisticated ﬁnancial architecture ofthe three, with its BRI formula creating a direct, contractually enforced link between the league's commercial success and player compensation.
What all three share: the recognition that broadcast rights are the ultimate value driver, that competitive balance (however imperfect) is commercially necessary, and that global reach — not just domestic dominance — is what separates a great league from an invincible one.
