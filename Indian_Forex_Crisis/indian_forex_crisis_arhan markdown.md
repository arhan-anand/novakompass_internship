# Indian Forex Crisis

Forex exchange refers to a country's foreign exchange reserves. These are the official assets held by the country's central bank to balance payments.

India's forex reserves have declined heavily recently due to the following reasons:

---

## 1. Oil Crisis

India imports over 85% of its oil requirements as the consumption demand of oil outpaces the production capacity. Due to the ongoing conflict in Iran, global prices of oil have risen considerably. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has shut down many supply lines. OPEC+, the cartel of oil producers, have also exercised production discipline, meaning that even as the price of oil rises, they do not increase the supply of oil, leading prices to increase even higher.

To shield consumers from these increasing prices, the government of India has cut special excise duty on petrol and diesel by ₹10, meaning that the government has reduced the tax it collects on petrol and diesel by ₹10, which prevents producers from passing this on to consumers. However, this comes at a cost. Reduced taxes mean lesser revenue for the government, which leads to an increased fiscal deficit.

If the government had not cut special excise duty on petrol and diesel, consumers would have had to pay more for them. As oil is used in so many industries, and especially transportation, various industries such as agriculture would suffer.

The government must look to increase the domestic production of oil and also switch to other forms of energy. It has also continued to buy discounted Russian crude oil.

---

## 2. Gold Imports

India also imports a lot of gold. Consumers demand gold for cultural purposes, as a form of financial security, and as a measure against inflation.

Global gold prices have increased due to a fear of recession, dollar concerns and central bank buying, which pushes the price up due to demand rising. This causes both the price of gold per unit to increase and a larger amount of gold to be demanded.

RBI also buys gold in exchange for dollars to strengthen the rupee by decreasing the economy's dependence on the dollar. However, gold is not as liquid as dollars. When the rupee is falling, the RBI intervenes by selling dollars in the open market (increasing its supply and therefore decreasing its value). However, it cannot do the same with gold.

The government could solve this by increasing the import duty on gold and providing alternative securities, like inflation-beating bonds.

---

## 3. Fertilizer Prices

India is an agriculture-based economy. This industry relies heavily on fertilizers. The demand for it exceeds the domestic supply. Therefore, fertilizer is imported in dollars.

Due to the conflict in Israel-Gaza, the supply lines for fertilizer have been disrupted. To protect farmers, the government provides subsidies on fertilizer, absorbing the increased cost, which reduces the government's revenue, increasing fiscal deficit.

To mitigate the effects of this crisis, the government must increase domestic production of fertilizer and enter into agreements with fertilizer exporting countries at fixed prices.

---

## 4. Foreign Investor Outflows

FPIs (Foreign Portfolio Investors) are a crucial source of dollar inflows for India. However, they have been exiting India recently.

Global risk-off sentiment has prompted foreign investors to leave India. As the rupee weakens, there is less incentive to invest in India as the foreign investors earn returns in rupees, which would be worth less as the rupee is weaker.

The price-to-earnings ratio of India is comparatively higher compared to similar countries (e.g. Brazil, Indonesia, and Vietnam). Therefore, FPIs exit Indian markets and enter those countries. Looking at India's rising inflation and the growing fiscal deficit, foreigners leave India as they recognize worsening economic conditions.

India must make investment attractive for FPIs. To do this, the government must keep real interest rates positive and improve ease of business. The rupee must be stabilized as well.
