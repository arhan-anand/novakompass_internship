## Executive Summary

India's foreign exchange reserves have declined by over $40 billion from their all-time peak of $728.5 billion in February 2026, falling to approximately $688 billion. 
This report examines the four primary drivers: elevated crude oil prices intensified by the West Asia conflict, record-high gold imports, surging fertilizer costs, and historic foreign investor outflows. The rupee has breached the ₹95/$ mark — a record low — and the merchandise trade deficit has hit an unprecedented $333 billion in FY2025-26. Together, these forces are straining India's external sector and challenging the RBI's ability to defend the currency.


---

## 1. Setting the Stage

India's foreign exchange ecosystem is under its most significant multi-front pressure in recent memory. After touching an all-time high of $728.5 billion in February 2026, reserves have declined sharply to approximately $688 billion — a drawdown of over $40 billion in roughly 12 weeks. The rupee breached ₹95 to the US dollar in March 2026, its weakest level in history, and the merchandise trade deficit hit a record $333 billion in FY2025-26 — a 17% rise over the prior year.

Four structural and cyclical forces are converging simultaneously:
1. Crude oil prices remain elevated due to geopolitical instability
2. Gold imports have nearly doubled in three years
3. Fertilizer costs have surged due to global supply shocks
4. Foreign institutional investors have pulled out at record pace

Each of these channels drains foreign currency, and together they are compressing India's ability to maintain exchange rate stability.

---

## 2. Crude Oil — The Largest Single Drain

India imports over 85% of its crude oil requirements, making it the world's third-largest oil importer. With Brent crude staying elevated due to the ongoing West Asia conflict — particularly uncertainty around Iran and the Strait of Hormuz — India's energy import bill has ballooned.

> **85%+** — Crude Oil Import Dependence. India meets over 85% of its petroleum needs through imports. Every $10 rise in crude oil price adds 35–40 basis points to domestic inflation and substantially increases forex outflows.

**Why prices are elevated:**
- The West Asia conflict has disrupted shipping routes and spiked risk premiums on crude futures, keeping Brent elevated.
- US-Iran tensions and Strait of Hormuz concerns continue to threaten supply routes critical to India's import pipeline.
- OPEC+ production discipline has limited supply elasticity, preventing prices from correcting sharply downward.

The domestic impact is compounding. The government has already cut special excise duty on petrol and diesel by ₹10 per litre each to provide consumer relief — but this comes at the cost of fiscal revenue. Simultaneously, higher energy costs ripple through logistics, manufacturing, and food supply chains, further straining the macroeconomic position.

---

## 3. Gold Imports — A Pure Dollar Drain

India has a deep cultural and economic relationship with gold, consistently ranking as one of the world's largest importers. Gold, however, is a pure foreign exchange outflow — it generates no productive return, creates no export earnings, and simply removes dollars from the country's financial system.

| Year | Gold Import Bill | Year-on-Year Change |
|---|---|---|
| FY 2022-23 | $35 billion | Baseline |
| FY 2023-24 | ~$45 billion | +29% |
| FY 2025-26 | $72 billion | +106% (vs FY23) |

**What is driving this surge?**
- Global gold prices have remained at elevated levels, driven by recession fears, dollar concerns, and central bank buying globally — causing both higher prices per unit and sustained consumer demand in India.
- The RBI itself has been buying gold to diversify its reserves, ironically spending dollar reserves to acquire gold that then cannot easily be deployed back into the forex market for rupee defense.
- Gold reserves within India's forex numbers — now at ~$113 billion — are non-deployable for market intervention, effectively reducing the usable forex firepower.

Prime Minister Modi made the extraordinary step of publicly urging citizens to reduce gold purchases — an appeal unprecedented even in the context of the 1991 balance of payments crisis, underscoring the severity of the current concern.

---

## 4. Fertilizer Prices — A Cascading Agricultural Shock

India's agricultural sector, the backbone of its rural economy, is heavily reliant on imported fertilizers — particularly urea. While domestic production exists, it falls significantly short of demand, and the shortfall is met through imports priced in US dollars.

> **+100%** — Urea Price Shock. Urea prices doubled between December 2025 and April 2026. Overall global fertilizer prices rose 46% in the same period, driven by West Asia supply disruptions and energy-intensive production costs.

**Key factors driving the fertilizer crisis:**
- India's fertilizer imports met 31–37% of domestic requirements over the past five years. In FY2025-26, this share is expected to cross 50% as domestic urea production fell short.
- Supply disruptions linked to the West Asia conflict have pushed India's fertilizer import bill up by nearly 80%, dramatically increasing both forex outflows and the government's already strained subsidy burden.
- Urea imports surged 60% in FY2025-26, placing enormous pressure on forex reserves at a time when other channels are simultaneously under stress.
- Fertilizer production is energy-intensive; rising LNG and natural gas prices have increased costs globally, making imported fertilizers more expensive on a structural basis.

The government's fertilizer subsidies, already one of the largest line items in the Union Budget, are expected to balloon further — creating a compounding fiscal and external sector problem. Higher subsidy spending increases the fiscal deficit, which in turn can weaken the rupee further, creating a self-reinforcing cycle.

---

## 5. Foreign Investor Outflows — A Historic Reversal

The withdrawal of foreign portfolio capital has been a defining feature of the current forex stress. Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) — who had been a consistent source of dollar inflows for nearly a decade — have reversed course at a pace not seen since India's liberalisation era.

| Period / Metric | FII / FPI Flow |
|---|---|
| Monthly avg, FY2015–2024 | +$3.7 billion (combined FDI+FII) |
| FY2025 onwards (monthly avg) | -$0.5 billion (net outflow) |
| March 2026 (single month) | ₹1.12 lakh crore ($12.1B) — worst month on record |
| Jan–May 2026 (cumulative) | ₹2.17 lakh crore — exceeds all of 2025 outflows |
| Full FY2026 FII outflow | $16.6 billion — highest since 1991 |

**Why are foreign investors leaving?**
- Global risk-off sentiment has driven capital back toward the US dollar and US treasuries, particularly as the Federal Reserve maintains a relatively hawkish posture compared to the RBI's more accommodative leanings.
- Rupee depreciation itself creates a negative feedback loop — as the rupee weakens, dollar-denominated returns on Indian equities decline for foreign investors, triggering further selling.
- Indian equity valuations, while corrected from their peaks, remain expensive on a relative basis compared to other emerging markets such as China, Brazil, and Southeast Asian economies.
- Macroeconomic headwinds including rising inflation, a widening current account deficit, and fiscal pressure have reduced India's attractiveness as a destination for foreign capital.

The FDI picture is also nuanced: while gross FDI inflows have remained strong at an average $7.3 billion/month in early 2026, outflows from disinvestments and outward FDI have averaged $5.7 billion/month, leaving net FDI significantly weaker than headline numbers suggest.

## 6. The Usable Reserves Problem

A critical nuance often missed in headline coverage is the distinction between stated forex reserves and usable forex reserves. India's headline reserves of approximately $688 billion appear robust — providing 11 months of import cover on paper. However, the actual deployable firepower is substantially lower.

| Reserve Component | Amount / Status |
|---|---|
| Total Stated Forex Reserves | ~$688 billion |
| Less: Gold holdings (non-deployable) | -$113 billion |
| Less: RBI forward book short positions | -$80–100 billion |
| **Effective Usable Reserves (est.)** | **~$480–500 billion** |
| Implied import cover (adjusted) | ~5.8 months — lowest since 2014 |

The RBI has also been running a buy-sell swap program — purchasing dollars now and committing to sell them 2–3 years later. Outstanding swap positions reportedly reached around $90 billion at end-March 2025. These committed future dollar sales further limit the RBI's room to manoeuvre. The result is that the RBI risks appearing a 'paper tiger' — with large stated reserves but limited ability to deploy them at scale when the rupee is under pressure.

---

## 7. Outlook & Risks

The convergence of these four pressures — oil, gold, fertilizers, and capital outflows — is not a transient phenomenon. Each has structural roots that will take time to address. The RBI faces a difficult trade-off between defending the rupee (requiring dollar sales that deplete reserves) and preserving reserve levels (allowing currency depreciation that risks inflation and further FII exit).

| Key Risk Factors | Potential Mitigants |
|---|---|
| Rupee approaching ₹100/$ | RBI intervention; rate differential management |
| Current account deficit widening | Export promotion; import substitution policies |
| Forward book commitments | Transparent unwinding of swap positions |
| Subsidy burden escalation | Domestic fertilizer capacity investment |
| West Asia conflict escalation | Diversified crude sourcing; strategic reserves |

In the medium term, reducing structural import dependence — particularly in energy and fertilizers — remains the most durable solution. Every $10 reduction in crude oil prices would save India approximately $13–15 billion annually in import costs, underscoring the outsized leverage that global energy markets hold over India's external sector.

India's $688 billion in reserves, while under pressure, still provides meaningful buffer compared to 2013 or 2022. The critical question is not whether India faces a balance of payments crisis, but how effectively policy can be calibrated to prevent further deterioration while growth is sustained.

---

> **The Bottom Line**
>
> India's forex challenge is a confluence of global commodity shocks, structural import dependence, and a global risk-off environment. Addressing it requires both short-term RBI intervention and long-term domestic capacity building in energy, agriculture, and capital market deepening.
